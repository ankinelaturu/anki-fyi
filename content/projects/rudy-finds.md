# Rudy Finds

A browser-native semantic search experiment focused on helping users rediscover information using meaning instead of exact keyword matching.

## What It Is

Rudy Finds explores a simple but surprisingly difficult problem:

People often remember what something meant, but they do not remember the exact words they used.

Traditional search systems are excellent when users know the right keyword, file name, page title, or phrase. The experience breaks down when users remember only the idea, topic, intent, or concept.

Rudy Finds investigates whether semantic retrieval can make discovery feel more natural by matching meaning instead of exact text.

The project explores browser-native search experiences where users can find relevant content from previously viewed pages, notes, documents, saved information, or personal knowledge collections using natural language. Rather than forcing users to remember where information lives, the system attempts to surface content based on conceptual similarity.

At its core, Rudy Finds is an experiment in helping users rediscover information they have already encountered but can no longer easily locate.

## Why I Built It

I kept noticing the same pattern in my own workflows.

I would remember reading something useful a few days earlier. I could remember the idea, the context, or the conclusion, but not the exact title or wording. Traditional search often failed because I was searching for meaning rather than keywords.

That observation led to a broader question:

What if search worked more like memory?

Instead of asking users to reconstruct the exact words used in a document, could a system help them find information based on concepts, relationships, and intent?

Rudy Finds was built to explore that idea.

The project also allowed me to investigate several themes that repeatedly appear throughout my work:

## Technical Challenges

The most difficult challenge is balancing relevance and explainability.

A semantic search system may return results that are technically related but not obviously connected from the user's perspective. If users cannot understand why a result appeared, trust quickly disappears.

Another challenge is scope.

What content should be indexed? How much context should be stored? How can search remain useful while respecting privacy and maintaining fast response times?

The project explores these questions while investigating how retrieval systems can feel intuitive rather than mysterious.

## Why It Matters

Rudy Finds represents a direction I find increasingly important: helping people navigate information rather than simply generating more information.

The modern challenge is often not access to content. It is finding the right content at the right moment.

Semantic retrieval has the potential to make discovery feel more natural by allowing users to search using ideas rather than exact wording.

The project also connects closely to several other efforts in my portfolio, including Ask Rudy, Lintern, and local-first AI experiments. Together they explore how retrieval, semantic understanding, and browser-native experiences can help people work with information more effectively.

The broader vision is simple:

Search should feel less like querying a database and more like asking your memory for help.

Rudy Finds explores what that future might look like.

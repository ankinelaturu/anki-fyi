# Ask Rudy

A conversational assistant for asking grounded questions about the content already in front of the user.

## What It Is

Ask Rudy explores a simple idea:

What if every page, document, note, or workspace could answer questions about itself?

Instead of acting as a general-purpose chatbot, Ask Rudy focuses on content-aware conversations. The assistant operates close to the information the user is currently viewing and attempts to answer questions using that context rather than relying entirely on model memory.

The project explores retrieval-based question answering experiences where users can interact with documents, articles, notes, saved content, browser pages, or personal knowledge collections through natural conversation.

Rather than asking users to manually search, skim, and piece together information, Ask Rudy attempts to act as a guide that helps users understand and navigate the content already available to them.

The goal is not to replace search. The goal is to make information easier to explore.

## Why I Built It

Many AI assistants require users to leave the content they are working with and move into a separate chat interface.

That workflow always felt backwards.

Users are often reading a page, researching a topic, studying material, reviewing documentation, or exploring information when a question appears. The assistant should be able to help directly within that context.

Ask Rudy was built to explore that idea.

I wanted to investigate what happens when AI becomes a companion to existing content instead of acting as an isolated destination.

The project also allowed me to explore several themes that repeatedly appear throughout my work:

- Retrieval-based AI systems
- Browser-native experiences
- Grounded answers
- Local-first AI concepts
- Explainable AI interactions
- Human-friendly knowledge exploration

- Generate grounded answers from available information.
- Navigate information through conversation instead of manual searching.

- Encourage source-aware answers.

- Support future citation and source-link experiences.
- Reduce friction between reading and understanding.

## Technical Challenges

One of the hardest problems is trust.

Users need confidence that answers are actually connected to the underlying content rather than being hallucinated by the model.

That requires careful retrieval, context selection, scope control, and answer generation.

Another challenge is knowing when not to answer.

A useful assistant should understand its boundaries. If the required information does not exist in the available content, the system should admit uncertainty rather than inventing a response.

Balancing helpfulness and reliability became one of the most interesting aspects of the project.

## Why It Matters

Ask Rudy represents a direction I find increasingly compelling: AI systems that help users understand information rather than simply generate information.

The project explores how retrieval, grounding, and contextual awareness can make AI more useful and trustworthy.

Conceptually, Ask Rudy also became one of the foundations for later experiments such as the Ask Anki experience inside this portfolio. Many of the same ideas appear in both systems:

- Retrieval before generation
- Grounded answers
- Clear scope boundaries
- Source-aware interactions
- Content-focused conversations

The broader idea is simple:

People should be able to talk to the information around them.

Ask Rudy explores what that future might look like.
